< Phobos
  • Type: Moon

  • Orbit Distance: Approximately 9,376 kilometers (5,827 miles) from Mars

  • Diameter: 22.4 kilometers (13.9 miles)

  • Day Length: Phobos has a very short day, completing an orbit around Mars in about 7 hours and 39 minutes.

  • Year Length: Phobos completes an orbit around Mars in about 0.3 Earth years (approximately 226.0 Earth days).

  • Moons: None (it is Earth's only Phobos)

  • Temperature: The average temperature on Phobos is around -4°C (25°F), but it can vary between -112°C (-170°F) and 27°C (80°F) depending on its position relative to the Sun.

Description:

Phobos, the larger and closer of Mars' two moons, is an irregularly shaped celestial body that orbits just 9,376 kilometers (5,827 miles) above the Martian surface, making it one of the closest orbiting moons in the solar system. Its surface is covered with craters, the largest being Stickney Crater, which dominates its rugged terrain. Phobos orbits Mars so rapidly that it rises and sets twice each Martian day. Scientists believe it may be a captured asteroid from the asteroid belt or debris from a larger body. Its surface temperatures vary widely, and it exhibits a dark, carbon-rich composition.

Unique Features and Fun Facts:

  1. Closest Orbiting Moon:

    Phobos orbits Mars at just 9,376 kilometers (5,827 miles) above the surface, making it the closest orbiting natural satellite in the solar system.

  2. Fast Orbit: Phobos orbits Mars faster than the planet rotates, rising in the west and setting in the east twice a day.

  3. Doomed Future: Due to tidal forces, Phobos is slowly spiraling toward Mars and is expected to either crash into the planet or break apart into a ring in about 50 million years.

  4. Unique Grooves:

    Phobos' surface is marked by mysterious linear grooves, believed to be caused by tidal forces or impact ejecta.

  5. Surface Composition: Phobos is dark and carbon-rich, with a surface similar to C-type asteroids.

  6. Low Gravity: The gravity on Phobos is so weak that a person could jump off its surface into space.

  7. Potential for Exploration: Phobos is a target for future Mars missions due to its proximity and potential as a stepping stone for Mars exploration.

Exploration and Missions:

  • First Mission – Phobos Program (1988):

    The Soviet Union launched the Phobos program, consisting of two probes, Phobos 1 and Phobos 2, to study Mars and its moons. Phobos 1 suffered a terminal failure en route to Mars, while Phobos 2 successfully entered Mars orbit and transmitted data on the Martian environment and Phobos before losing contact.












  • Recent Observation – Perseverance Rover (2024):

    Perseverance rover captured the silhouette of Phobos as it transited the Sun on September 30, 2024. These observations help scientists refine their understanding of Phobos' orbit and its gradual inward spiral toward Mars.










  • Upcoming Mission – Martian Moons eXploration (MMX):

    The Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) is developing the MMX mission, aiming to launch in the mid-2020s. The mission plans to collect surface material from Phobos and return it to Earth, providing insights into the moon's origin and the early solar system.








Fun Fact!
Phobos is so small and dark that if you were standing on the Martian surface, it would look more like a fast-moving star than a traditional moon. It takes only about 4 hours to cross the sky from rise to set!
